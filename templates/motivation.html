Face-Off Motivation

Online Face Recognition

  • Many online platforms use user uploaded photos in face recognition systems.
  • This includes most social media services (Facebook, Instagram, etc.)
  • Oftentimes data is leaked or scraped and can be used in harmful or controversial applications.
  • Examples include:
    - Clearview.ai scraping Facebook photos without user consent for use in their law enforcement software.
    - Cambridge Analytica collecting Facebook profile data without consent.
    - Facebook's face recognition system violating Illinois biometric privacy laws.

Hiding Faces From AI

  • Face-Off allows users to protect their face data by adding a discrete and strategic distortion layer which hides their face from being recognized by face recognition.
  • Note: Face-Off does not guarantee protection, it increases the chances that a face recognition system will not be able to correctly recognize a face.
  • Note: Face-Off does not stop face detection, a service will still know that a face is there, it just won't know who it belongs to.

Face-Off Drawbacks

  • The faces generated by Face-Off have obvious perturbation artifacts, and users may not be comfortable uploading these images.
  • The adversarial examples generated rely the transferability property of adversarial attacks. By amplifying the noise mask with a constant, this improves the likelihood of transfer and results in an unappealing image.

Image Scaling Attacks

  • A recent work with image scaling attacks addresses these issues perfectly.
  • They deconstruct scaling algorithms (nearest, linear, cubic, lanczos) to mask smaller images inside high resolution images.
  • Downscaling these attacked images result in the smaller "target" image being revealed to machine learning pipelines.
